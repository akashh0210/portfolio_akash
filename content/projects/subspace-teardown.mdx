## Problem

Subspace.money is India's subscription-sharing marketplace — a genuinely novel product category. But novelty alone doesn't drive retention. I did this teardown to stress-test whether the product's current execution matched the ambition of its positioning.

The question: where is Subspace leaving trust, revenue, and retention on the table right now?

## Hypothesis

Most early-stage consumer marketplaces underinvest in three things: trust signals (so users share with strangers), feature merchandising (so users discover non-obvious value), and GTM copy alignment (so the homepage matches what the product actually does). Subspace likely has all three gaps.

## What I built

A full product teardown structured around five product pillars, using SWOT analysis and Porter's Five Forces as frameworks. The output is an 18-page report with embedded product screenshots and competitive references.

**Five gaps identified:**

1. **Home screen identity sprawl.** The homepage tries to speak to both sharers and joiners simultaneously, producing copy that convinces neither. The primary CTA competes with a secondary one above the fold.

2. **Admin rating system signal loss.** The admin role carries real trust weight in a subscription-sharing context — admins gatekeep group access and handle disputes. The current rating system doesn't surface admin quality in a way that a new user can act on.

3. **Opaque refund logic.** Refund scenarios in subscription-sharing are genuinely complex (what happens if one member stops paying?). The current flow buries this. Every unresolved refund question converts a potential user into a support ticket.

4. **Unmerchandised Rentals pillar.** The Rentals feature is structurally different from subscription-sharing but is visually identical in the nav. Users scroll past a genuinely useful feature because it looks like a sub-category of what they've already seen.

5. **GTM copy mismatches.** Three separate surfaces (App Store listing, homepage headline, onboarding) describe the product differently. For a new category, consistency is the trust layer.

## Outcome & learning

Submitted for Vocallabs.ai Product Intern assignment (May 2026). The main learning: teardowns are most useful when they go beyond "this is bad" to "here is the specific user behaviour that makes this costly." Each gap in this report includes a user-level consequence and a proposed fix prioritised by effort-to-impact ratio.

The Porter's Five Forces pass was the most counterintuitive part: Subspace's real competitive threat isn't another sharing marketplace — it's users individually negotiating shared accounts informally. The product has to beat a WhatsApp group + Splitwise, which is a different design brief than beating a direct competitor.
